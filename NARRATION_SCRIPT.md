## SECTION 3: Evolution of AI in Chemistry (1.5 minutes)

Now let's trace how AI in chemistry has evolved. This didn't happen overnight.

In the 1980s and 90s, we had rule-based expert systems‚Äîbasically, chemists wrote down rules and computers followed them. Simple, but inflexible.

By the 2000s, statistical machine learning emerged. Models learned patterns from data, but still needed humans to design features.

The real revolution came after 2012 with deep learning. Neural networks started learning features automatically. This is when AlphaFold began changing protein science.

Today, in 2024, we have Foundation Models and Large Language Models like ChemDFM that can reason about chemistry in natural language. It's a completely different paradigm.

## SECTION 4: System Prompts (1 minute)

Speaking of language models, let me explain something crucial: system prompts.

A system prompt is the initial set of instructions given to an AI before it interacts with users. Think of it as "personality programming" that shapes how the AI responds.

For a chemical LLM, the system prompt might say: "Always conserve mass. Follow valency rules. Carbon forms 4 bonds. Prioritize non-toxic reagents. Cite your confidence level."

According to arXiv paper 2505.07027, system prompts are CRITICAL for ensuring chemical validity. Without them, an AI might suggest reactions that violate fundamental chemistry laws‚Äîlike creating atoms out of nothing!

LLM-augmented systems like "LLM-Syn-Planner" from arXiv 2505.07027 use evolutionary search combined with LLM reasoning to generate and optimize complete **retrosynthetic pathways** in minutes.

## SECTION 8: Retrosynthesis (1 minute)

Finally, let's talk about retrosynthesis‚Äîone of the most exciting AI applications in chemistry.

Retrosynthesis is the art of working BACKWARDS. You start with a complex target molecule and break it down step-by-step into simpler, commercially available starting materials.

The breakthrough from arXiv 2510.16590 is "Atom-Anchored LLMs". These models assign UNIQUE identifiers to every atom in a molecule and track them through the reaction.

This atom-level reasoning achieves over 90% accuracy in identifying where reactions should happen, and 74% accuracy in predicting the final reactants. No atoms are lost or magically created.
